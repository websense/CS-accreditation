Justification of Program

The Bachelor of Advanced Computer Science (Honours) (BACS) is a four-year integrated honours degree for high achieving students who have attained an ATAR of at least 92 and a pass in ATAR Maths Methods (or the UWA equivalent MATHS1721). There is a progress requirement for BACS of a weighted average mark of at least 70 to progress to the honours year. BACS is structured on 4 components: a Computer Science core (12 units); advanced study of a specialist discipline through a major leading to an honours year with research project (10-12 units); contextual knowledge such as philosophy and ethics for AI (2-4 units); and free electives (4-6 units). BACS graduates are equipped for industry or academic research roles, to start-up new technology companies, and to contribute at an advanced level to the many businesses looking to harness the potential of AI, Data Science, Cybersecurity and Quantum Computing. The Computer Science core of BACS develops knowledge of the principles of computation and information that underpin the computing software and systems that are revolutionising the world in which we live, work and communicate.

In the Quantum Computing major students learn strong computing and quantum computing skills through foundational building to advanced units. Students study algorithms, computer systems, high performance computing, physics, maths, cybersecurity, and philosophy.

Graduates of the Bachelor of Advanced Computer Science [Honours] (Quantum Computing) will have completed: 16 mandatory ICT units, 1 level-3 elective chosen from 3 ICT units (including a WIL unit), 5 Physics and supporting Maths units, and a 4-unit ICT research project.

The majors of the Bachelor of Advanced Computing and BPhil Honours contain all the units of the Computer Science major and additionally have a specialist stream including an honours year with a 24-point research project in the students’ area of specialisation. Therefore the "capstone" for these programs comprises two parts: CITS3200 for software developer outcomes and CITS4010-11 for advanced specialisation. Together these units provide and assess the integration of knowledge and skills specifically targeted at the professional roles identified for each program.

Undergraduate students learn about professional practice throughout their degree. Learning activities include guest lectures from industry, research essays, reflective practice, and extra-curricular events including club-run events such as Industry Insights. Students complete a final-semester, team-based project conducted in conjunction with a real industry client, together with industry-led workshops on professional skills, providing a structured learning experience to prepare graduates for professional practice. Undergraduate students may also choose the internship-based work integrated learning unit CITS3009 as an elective. CITS3009 supports students to apply, enhance, and develop generic and/or specific skills gained through their Computer Science studies in an industry context.
