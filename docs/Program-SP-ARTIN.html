Justification of Program

The Master of Information Technology (MIT) equips students with advanced IT skills. The MIT introduces systematic approaches to select, develop, apply, integrate and administer secure computing technologies to enable users to accomplish their personal, organizational, and societal goals. The conversion program is designed for students and professionals who have a tertiary degree in another discipline (such as arts, science, marketing, finance, or business) and who seek to develop specialist skills in computing and software engineering. It is designed for students and returning professionals who wish to change direction or expand their skill set to pursue a career in IT. The MIT comprises 16 units (at least 12 ICT units) in 4 groups: Conversion, Core, Specialisation and Electives. The 4-unit Conversion provides foundational skills in programming, database and cybersecurity. The 4-unit IT Core comprises software requirements and design and web development, a philosophy unit on communication skills and computing ethics and a capstone group project. The Electives include a WIL unit, offering structured, quality internships with not-for-profit, community and government organisations locally, regionally, and globally. Students are selected for the WIL unit based on interview with the industry clients. The Electives also include a 2-unit research project for students (prerequisite WAM 70+) offering a pathway from the MIT into PhD studies and cutting-edge industries. The MIT has three Specialisations: AI, Software Systems and Applied Computing. These Specialisations introduced in 2025 formalise unit selections that many students were already choosing from the more flexible prior structure of the MIT.

The Artificial intelligence specialisation equips students with skills and knowledge to understand, evaluate, design and implement artificial intelligence systems with hands-on practice in a variety of contemporary artificial intelligence techniques, from machine learning and deep learning to natural language processing and adaptive systems that can learn from, and adapt to, a changing and unknown environment. Students develop in-demand skills and leadership qualities for an exciting career in AI.

Graduates of each of the specialisations of the Master of Information Technology course will have completed at least 11 ICT and 1 ICT-related units comprising: 4 foundation ICT units (with a choice between 2 units at level 2) taken by students with a non-cognate undergraduate degree, 3 compulsory core ICT units and one core ICT-related communications and ethics unit, 4 specialist core ICT units, and 4 elective units selected from 17 level 4 and 5 ICT units (including a WIL unit) and 6 ICT-related elective units.

CITS5017 Deep Learning Practical projects CITS5017 builds on knowledge of machine learning through the sequence CITS1401 and CITS5508. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA MLNG (level 4) by assessing the suitability of various deep neural network models for specific tasks, to engineer and optimise models, to evaluate, analyse and troubleshoot their solutions. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students create, analyse and evaluate multilayer perceptrons, deep convolutional neural networks, recurrent neural networks, autoencoders, transformers, deep reinforcement learning, generative models and generative adversarial networks. Students demonstrate their understanding of different initialisation strategies of neural network weights, various activation functions, and learning rate schedulers. In addition, students demonstrate that they grasp the concept of these networks' strengths and weaknesses as well as application problems suitable for them. (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
Individual practical projects give students hands-on experience of advanced topics in the deep neural networks mentioned above. Students use the TensorFlow library for their Python code. Students learn to set up Python environment for running their code as well as submit batch jobs on GPU clusters. These hands-on experiences allow them to acquire in-depth knowledge of how these deep neural works, how to fine-tune them, how their performances are evaluated and enhanced (1,3,8). (Complex computing criteria)
CITS5206 Information Technology Capstone Project Group project CITS5206 builds on knowledge of software development through the sequence CITS1401, CITS1003, CITS2005 or CITS2002, CITS4401, and CITS5505. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA PROG (level 4) by designing, coding, verifying, testing, documenting, amending and refactoring a complex program for an industry client. The project requires students to contribute to the selection of the software development methods, tools, techniques, and security practices, and apply agreed standards, tools, and security measures to achieve well-engineered outcomes. During the project, students review their own work and team members' work. These skills are assessed in the group project deliverables and through individual reflections, professionalism and peer assessment. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students desmonstrate that they can create a complex software product, critically analyse requirements and resources, assess social impacts and their professional responsibilities. (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
The group project involves external stakeholders (6) and requirements identification (9) and the solution requires the use of in-depth computing, domain knowledge and an analytical approach (3) and comprises many component parts (8). (Complex computing criteria)
CITS5505 Agile Web Development Web Application Project CITS5505 builds on knowledge of programming, databases and systems through the sequence CITS1401, CITS1402, CITS2002 or CITS2005. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA PROG (level 4) by designing, coding, verifying, and deploying web applications using a range of technologies, including HTML, CSS, JavaScript, AJAX, jQuery, Bootstrap, Python (Flask), SQLAlchemy, and SQLite. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students select frameworks, evaluate the quality of web applications and create their own full-stack web application (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
The group project requires them to build their own full-stack web application, and their responsibilities include selection of frameworks, designing web APIs, implementing authentication and security mechanisms, testing and finally deploying the application. Students work in groups of four, review both their own and their peers' code via the version control system Git, and provide regular formal project deliverables and demonstrations to the course facilitators. The group project has a high number of interdependent parts (8), and requires a deep level of knowledge (3). (Complex computing criteria)
CITS5508 Machine Learning Assessed laboratory exercises CITS5508 builds on foundational knowledge of data science and machine learning through the sequence CITS1401, STAT1400, STAT2401-03 and CITS2402 or CITS4009. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA MLNG (level 3) and DATS (level 4) by investigating data science problems and datasets through two machine learning projects. They apply diverse techniques from supervised and unsupervised machine learning. They demonstrate systematic knowledge of techniques through hypotheses, evaluation and risk modelling. Students apply established supervised and unsupervised learning techniques in their assessed lab exercises. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students prepare data, train, optimse and validate models and monitor model performance. (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6). Specifically in the projects, students build and train an ML system using a real-world dataset, selecting from a range of technologies. The project requires students to research (2,3) and implement novel solutions (5,9), and validate the solution (4,8). (Complex computing criteria)
Unit Code Unit Name Notes in support of Claim
CITS5206 Information Technology Capstone Project Capstone team project with a real industry client and software professional mentor. Assessed on delivered project quality, professional portfolio including reflection on professional practice.

MIT students learn about professional practice throughout their degree. Learning activities include guest lectures from industry, research essays, reflective practice, and extra-curricular events including club-run activities such as the Industry Insights event. MIT students complete a final-semester, team-based project conducted in conjunction with a real industry client, together with industry-led workshops on professional skills, providing a structured learning experience to prepare graduates for professional practice. MIT students may also choose the internship-based work integrated learning unit SVLG5001 as an elective. As well as providing ICT-related work experience, SVLG5001 projects with not-for-profit, community and government organisations locally, regionally, and globally prompt critical reflection about civic engagement.
