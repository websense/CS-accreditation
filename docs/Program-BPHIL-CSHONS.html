Justification of Program

The Computer Science majortaken in the Bachelor of Science or the Bachelor of Philosophy [Honours] provides students with the knowledge and skills required to directly contribute to the application and analysis of computing technologies in industry and society. Through the 12 ICT units of the major graduates attain deep knowledge of theoretical, algorithmic, implementation and systems’ principles that underpin databases, programming languages, and internet-based systems. The Computer Science major covers what may be thought of as traditional computer science. The major has a strong emphasis on models of computation, programming, and systems from both a theoretical and a practical standpoint.

Graduates of the Bachelor of Philosophy [Honours] (Computer Science major and Honours in Computer Science and Software Engineering) will have completed 12 ICT units: 11 mandatory ICT units and 1 level-3 elective chosen from 6 ICT units (including a WIL unit) in the Computer Science major. In addition in the honours year they will complete 4 level-4 or level-5 ICT units and a 4-unit ICT research project.

CITS3001 Advanced Algorithms Project assessments CITS3001 builds on skills developed in the sequence CITS1401 and CITS2200 with CITS2211 providing foundation of fundamental models for the design and analysis of computing systems. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA PROG (programming/software development level 3) by designing, coding, verifying, testing, documenting, amending and refactoring moderately complex programs/scripts. They analyse correctness, complexity, efficiency and apply problem-solving techniques to achieve a well engineered result. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students analyse and evaluate advanced algorithms, and use problem-motivated design to create novel algorithms (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
In the project they address complex computing problems through challenging algorithm design questions that involve abstracting a problem and developing a novel algorithm from first principles (Criteria 2,3,4,5,8). The project assessment requires students to solve novel algorithmic problems to a high degree of mathematic rigour. This requires a good knowledge of algorithms and the tools of complexity and correctness analysis. It also involves conceptual and abstract thinking to apply these to an unseen problem. It can also involve creativity to extrapolate these foundations into new ways to approach problems. (Complex computing criteria)
CITS3002 Computer Networks Team practical project CITS3002 builds on knowledge of operating systems, network architecture and cyber-physical systems, and software through the sequence CITS1003, CITS1401 and CITS2002. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA NTDS (level 3) by designing specific network components using agreed architectures, design standards, patterns and methodology, translating logical designs into physical designs that meet specified operational parameters for capacity and performance, validation and error correction procedures, and contributing to the development of recovery routines and contingency procedures, alternative network architectures, networking topologies and design options. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students create distributed applications for computer networks and evaluate protocols, scalabililty, network performance, security, and privacy (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
The team practical project involves conflicting technical issues (1), has no obvious solution and requires conceptual thinking and innovative analysis to formulate suitable abstract models (2), is a high-level problem possibly including many component parts or subproblems (8) and the cause of problems are unknown (9) (Complex computing criteria)
CITS3200 Professional Computing Team Project CITS3200 builds on knolwedge of software development through the sequence CITS1401, CITS1003, CITS2200, CITS2005 and CITS3403. (Prerequisite knowledge)
Students develop skills in SFIA PROG (programming/software development level 4) in teams that design, code, verify, test, document, amend and refactor a complex program for an industry client. The project requires them to contribute to the selection of the software development methods, tools, techniques, and security practices, and apply agreed standards, tools, and security measures to achieve well-engineered outcomes. During the project, students review their own work and colleagues' work. (SFIA knowledge)
These skills are assessed in the group project deliverables and through individual reflections, professionalism and peer assessment where students analyse and evaluate the quality of their products and processes as they create a significant software product (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
The project involves external stakeholders (6) needs requirements identification (9) and the solution requires the use of in-depth computing, domain knowledge and an analytical approach (3) and comprises many component parts (8). (Complex computing criteria)
CITS3403 Agile Web Development Web Application Project CITS3403 builds on knowledge of programming, databases and systems through the sequence CITS1401 (Prerequisite knowledge), CITS1402, CITS2002.
Students develop skills in SFIA PROG (level 4) by designing, coding, verifying, and deploying web applications using a range of technologies, including HTML, CSS, JavaScript, AJAX, jQuery, Bootstrap, Python (Flask), SQLAlchemy, and SQLite. (SFIA knowledge)
In the written and practical assessments students select frameworks, evaluate the quality of web applications and create their own full-stack web application (Analyse, evaluate, create assessments Bloom levels 4,5,6).
The group project requires them to build their own full-stack web application, and their responsibilities include selection of frameworks, designing web APIs, implementing authentication and security mechanisms, testing and finally deploying the application. Students work in groups of four, review both their own and their peers' code via the version control system Git, and provide regular formal project deliverables and demonstrations to the course facilitators. The group project has a high number of interdependent parts (8), and requires a deep level of knowledge (3). (Complex computing criteria)

The majors of the Bachelor of Advanced Computing and BPhil Honours contain all the units of the Computer Science major and additionally have a specialist stream including an honours year with a 24-point research project in the students’ area of specialisation. Therefore the "capstone" for these programs comprises two parts: CITS3200 for software developer outcomes and CITS4010-11 for advanced specialisation. Together these units provide and assess the integration of knowledge and skills specifically targeted at the professional roles identified for each program.

Undergraduate students learn about professional practice throughout their degree. Learning activities include guest lectures from industry, research essays, reflective practice, and extra-curricular events including club-run events such as Industry Insights. Students complete a final-semester, team-based project conducted in conjunction with a real industry client, together with industry-led workshops on professional skills, providing a structured learning experience to prepare graduates for professional practice. Undergraduate students may also choose the internship-based work integrated learning unit CITS3009 as an elective. CITS3009 supports students to apply, enhance, and develop generic and/or specific skills gained through their Computer Science studies in an industry context.
